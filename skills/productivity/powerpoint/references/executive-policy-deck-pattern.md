# Executive / Policy Deck Pattern

Use for regulatory, policy, board, strategy, or public-sector recommendation decks.

## Structure
1. **Answer-first executive summary** — but do not collapse the whole deck into one recommendation if options are requested.
2. **Context / trigger** — what changed and why the decision is needed now.
3. **Design constraints** — principles or architecture that must not be broken.
4. **Evidence / peer scan** — concise comparator findings; one conclusion per slide.
5. **Evaluation criteria** — 4–6 criteria that explain how options will be judged.
6. **Options** — each option gets a consistent slide: what it is, strengths, limitations, when it works.
7. **Decision matrix / trade-off view** — show why one option leads while others remain viable.
8. **Recommended package** — only after options are visible.
9. **Roadmap / sequencing** — what to do now, next, later.
10. **Appendix / sources** — dense citations belong here or in notes.

## Visual style
- Prefer restrained consulting/enterprise design: white or near-black background, one accent color, sharp rectangles, strong hierarchy.
- Avoid decorative gradients and generic AI/SaaS pastel card grids for serious policy decks.
- Use action titles: conclusion or decision point, not topic labels.
- Keep key body text large enough to read in slide-sorter/contact-sheet view.

## QA checklist
- Are multiple options genuinely presented before the recommendation?
- Can a reader understand the trade-offs without listening to narration?
- Does the decision matrix communicate both the preferred option and credible alternatives?
- Are dense research details moved to appendix/notes?
- Do contact sheets reveal any tiny text, low contrast, or repetitive card layouts?
